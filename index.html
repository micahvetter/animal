NARWHALS.
The mysterious unicorns of the sea.

5 Must Knows

Narwhal Biology

The tusk is a hollow tooth with a spiral pattern.

The ivory tusk grows right through the narwhal's upper lip. These tusks can impressively get up to 2.7 m long; although not all narwhals have them. Minus the tusk, adult male narwhals can measure up to 5 m long. Like the beluga whales, narwhals lack a dorsal fin and instead have a dorsal ridge.

Coloration

When first born, Narwhal calves are usually blotchy gray. As they get older, Narwhals usually have a black and white spotted coloration on the dorsal region of their body, while some may be almost completely white.

Narwhal Taxonomy

Narwhals are just medium sized whales.

The scientific name of Narwhals is Monodon monoceros. They are closely related to the beluga whale. Both of these species are the only extant members of the family Monodontidae, otherwise known as the "white whales".

Narwhal Behavior

Despite their large bodies, they are very lazy animals.

Narwhals are seen swimming slowly in shallow waters but can dive up to a mile deep and much faster when needed.

Sociability

Narwhals typically travel in groups of 10-15. The groups consist of adult males, mothers, and their children.

Reproduction

Mating takes place belly to belly, often in the spring. The females give birth to a calf around 14 months later, and then do not mate again for nearly 3 years.

Diet

When eating, they create a vaccuum suction with their mouths. Some of the animals they ingest include; arctic cod, cuttlefish, polar cod, squid, and shrimp.

Narwhals And Humans

The conservation status of Narwhals is nearly threatened.

In the 60's and 70's humans began trying to capture Narwhals and put them into captivity. However, the animals did not endure captivity well and most died within several months. Since then, no Narwhals have been placed in captivity.

Narwhal Ecology

Narwhals are rarely found far from ice.

Unlike many of their fellow whale species, Narwhals can be found in the Arctic waters of Canada, Greenland, Norway, and Russia. In the winter, Narwhals can often be spotted in deep waters, usually beneath icepacks where they can live for up to five months. Once it becomes warmer in the summer they migrate to shallower waters, which usually have much less ice.

In Summary...

Narwhals truly are mysterious creatures. Hiding beneath the ice has made them incredibly hard to track and observe. Their large tusks set them apart from other sea life and has captivated the wonder of humans for centuries.
